The cost of international peace




The Security Council can authorise a range of peaceful and coercive measures when a country presents a conflict such as conflict prevention, peacemaking, peacekeeping, peace enforcement and peacebuilding. Usually, a peacekeeping operation (PKO) is designed When the fight has ended to preserve peace and implement agreements between the peacemakers.




The United Nations has 8 billion dollars every year for peacekeeping operations. This year, the Security Council has approved 15 missions with a budget of $8.27 billion. However, as of 30 June 2015, Member States owed approximately $1.6 billion in current and back peacekeeping dues.

Deploying a Peacekeeping Operation


Who decides it?

The Security Council determines the existence of a threat to the peace and recommends what, when and where an action should be taken. There are 15 members and five of them are veto-wielding Permanent Members (China, France, Russia, the United Kingdom and the United States). In order to pass a resolution, nine votes -and none of the Permanent Members voting against- are required.


How does it work?

When there is a cisis between countries, either a Member of the Security Council or the Secretary-General can ask the Security Council to debate the situation. If it's a risk to international piece, the Security Council will ask the Secretary-General to analyse and assess the overall security, political, humanitarian, and military situation and the future implications of the mission through a report called the Technical Assessment Mission (TAM). These findings are sent to the Security Council, who will authorise the deployment of the PKO as a resolution form and write a mandate with all the tasks and objectives of the mission.

Missions decided by 15 countries


João Maurício has been working in peace operations for more than eight years, and now he is the Deputy Operations Officer in the Brazilian Engineering Company in the United Nations Stabilization Mission in Haiti. He explains that decisions of approving -or not- PKOs are totally political. 15 representatives (and 5 veto-members) from the Security Council decide the international presence -or not- in a specific country: "Going through the history, you will see loads of resolutions that didn't pass because one or two Permanent Member didn't want it".


Eduarda Hamann,coordinator of the Peacebuilding programm at the Instituto Igaratpé in Brasil, says that PKOs are created and work in the middle of a "huge competition of political power". There is a big division between the countries that create the mandates, those who pay for the mandates and those who implement them. This represents 20-30 countries out of 193 members, while the Secretary General receives demands and pushes from all the members: "It is not an easy math".


João also goes through the lasts budgets to explain how missions such as in Haiti, Liberia, Mali or Africa Central Republic are being downsized because of France or the United States, present in the Security Council and the General Assembly. Two models of peacekeeping operations


The United Nations needs the consent of the parties to the conflict in order to deploy a PKO. Otherwise, the action is typically categorised as a peace enforcement mission. This agreement between the host country, the actors involved and the United Nations, can either be a Status of Forces Agreement (SOFA) [pdf] or a Status of Mission Agreement (SOMA) for missions with unarmed personnel only.


When the Security Council approves the resolution, asks which State Members want to assist the mission. This decision is, again, political. Countries will choose to intervene -or not- depending on the relationship with the host country. If a country agrees, personnel, equipment and services are provided voluntary, even though the United Nations will reimburse a fix amount of money to pay the troops.The basis of this agreement is set by the Memorandum of Understanding (MOU) Finally, the United Nations prepares the Rules of Engagement (ROE) between the UN, the host country and the government that assists. This non-public document defines the use of force of a specific mandate of the misison and situation on the ground.


There are two models of peacekeeping operations: an interim measure to help manage a conflict by two or more parties, known as traditional missions, and multidimensional missions, which act once a peace agreement is in place with two or more components (civilian, military, police). João Maurício explains that peacekeeping missions experienced a change after the intervention of the UN in Rwanda. In 1993,Rwanda and Uganda requested the deployment of military observers to prevent the military use of the area by RPF. However, the United Nations and the whole international community remained accused of not having prevented the genocide.

Traditional peacekeeping operations

This was the original operation during the Cold Ward, aiming to observe the ceasefire and separation of forces. They assist by negotiation, persuasion and verification of the situation wihtout playing a role in political efforts. Some examples are the United Nations Peacekeeping Force in Cyprus (UNFICYP) or The United Nations Mission for the Referendum in Western Sahara (MINURSO), which was established in 1991 and monitores the ceasefire and reduces risks while the country waits for a referendum. Multidimensional peacekeeping operations

This has been the most common form of PKO after the Cold War.Once the traditional operation is over and there is a peace agreement, even if it's fragile, military, civilian, and police forces assist to a secure environment while working with national authorities.


Example of a multidimensional operations are the former United Nations Mission in the Democratic Republic of Congo (MONUC), now MONUSCO, or the United Nations Stabilization Mission in Haiti (MINUSTAH).

Who pays for peacekeeping operations?


Even though the Security Council defines the size and the mandate of the resoltion, the General Assembly is the one who approves the budget and resources of the mission. At the end of June each year, the General Assembly collects the approved resolutions and establishes the budget that will start the first day of July. According to the Article 17 of the Charter of the United Nations, every Member State is legally obligated to pay their respective share towards peacekeeping missions. However, countries can also voluntarily support these missions in the form of transportation, supplies, personnel and financial contributions.


For the fiscal year 2015/16, the General Assembly approved a budget of $8.27 billion [pdf] for UN Peacekeeping operations. A mandatory share

Member states established a special scale to contribute to peacekeeping expenses depending on the relative economic wealth among other things. The 5 permanent members of the Security Council are required to pay a larger share because of their duty to keep the international peace and security.
